Teaching the "Long" 18th Century - A Conversation & Resources

This roundtable brought together scholars from a broad array of geographical foci and institutional perspectives who have been at the forefront of efforts to rethink approaches to thinking, researching, and, crucially, teaching the art and material culture of an interconnected “long” eighteenth century. The aim was to reflect upon teaching in light of renewed debates on the reparation of objects, revision of histories, and inclusion of colonized and enslaved voices in museums, plantation sites, and public squares. We questioned the stakes of a “long” eighteenth century from different vantage points as we account for the histories of colonialism, enslavement, and extraction of bodies and lands, objects and knowledge. And, crucially, we asked how our inquiry shifts when we think from our pedagogical sites. We were keen to bring into our orbit innovative pedagogical practices because we believe that pedagogy is a core intellectual project, and because it has real ramifications for the future of our discipline. So how have we approached the “long” and “broad” eighteenth century in our classrooms and research clusters?

Participants were invited to anchor their short presentations by way of a salient episode: a syllabus, a course session, an assignment, or a digital project. The objective was not to achieve a comprehensive account of the varied perspectives and approaches; instead, we look at this roundtable as part of many such discussions that are already taking place and that will continue in the future. Our pedagogical sites, the animating force for this discussion, have manifested on our Zoom screens during a period that has seen the brutal violence and deaths of individuals reverberate globally, from renewed calls for racial justice following George Floyd’s murder in Minneapolis, Minnesota, to the devastation wrought by the COVID-19 pandemic. Through the past year we have often inhabited, and continue to inhabit, non-intersecting physical spaces, disparate temporal realities, desperate emergencies, burdening limitations, unseen disabilities, and variously exhausted bodies. All of our speakers in their pedagogical work bring the ethics of care and justice at its core which inspires us, and we hope it will inspire you.


To learn more about each of these episodes and discover open-access resources to use in your own teaching, browse through the grid below. Explore objects and their entangled histories on the ArtHX: Visual and Medical Legacies of British Colonialism, the website collaboratively designed by Anna Arabindon-Kesson and her students. Find the syllabus Nebahat Avcıoğlu designed by for her innovative graduate seminar “Scrutinizing the Western Gaze: The Turk in 18th Century European Art," which resulted in a collectively produced bibliographic article on “Turquerie” for Oxford Bibliographies Online. Scroll through the website Travelling Objects: European Art and the Wider World, a supplemental resource for the textbook Art, Commerce, and Colonialism, 1600-1800 edited by Emma Barker. Learn about “Pathways to Art History,” an initiative conceived of by Ananda Cohen-Aponte who partnered with the Multicultural Resource Center to engage undergraduate students enrolled in her Latin American survey course. Check out the syllabus for the undergraduate seminar “Africa in the Indian Ocean World,” which Prita Meier designed to focus on matter and materiality. Engage with the interactive mapping project created by Nancy Um for her graduate course, “Art History in the Digital Age.” And discover the extensive syllabus Stephen Whiteman produced for the year-long MA course “Beijing and Beyond: Art and Empire in Early Modern China.” You can also watch to lively discussion from the Q&A with speakers.
